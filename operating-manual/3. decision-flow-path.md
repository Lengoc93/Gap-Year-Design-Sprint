### Door Type — How to tell which one you're in

| | One-way door | Two-way door |
|---|---|---|
| **Definition** | Once you walk through, the pre-decision state is gone forever | You can try it, learn, and reverse with low cost |
| **Examples** | Marriage + relocation, having a child, quitting without a return offer, telling a secret | Trying a new routine, taking a freelance project, testing a hypothesis schedule, investing in an ETF |
| **The trick** | Some decisions *look* two-way but are actually one-way because of social/emotional cost of reversing | Some decisions *feel* one-way but are actually two-way — the ENFP-7 fear of being trapped makes reversible things feel permanent |
| **Biggest risk** | Deciding *badly* | Deciding *slowly* |
| **Time to decide** | Take what you need — but set a deadline so "taking time" doesn't become avoidance | Decide fast, learn by doing |
| **Protocol** | Full: all 9 compass Qs, full analysis, deep body check, hard commit with no-revisit trigger | Light: 3 compass Qs, quick analysis, gut check, experiment commit with review date |

## Compass Check — The 9 Questions

### Sovereignty (root, 9.0)

| # | Question | What it catches |
|---|----------|----------------|
| S1 | **Am I choosing this, or am I choosing to be seen choosing this?** | The validation loop — performing freedom for someone else's approval |
| S2 | **After I commit — can I change my mind without anyone's permission?** | The trap — will someone hold it over me? Will leaving feel like failure because of *their* expectation? |
| S3 | **If I say no to this, what am I actually afraid of losing?** | The hidden dependency — when "no" feels dangerous even though you're free |

### Diversification (strategy, 8.5)

| # | Question | What it catches |
|---|----------|----------------|
| D1 | **Does this open a genuinely new door, or does it let me avoid walking through one I've already opened?** | Shiny object disguised as strategy — avoidance of commitment |
| D2 | **If I add this, what do I have to hold more loosely — and am I actually okay with that?** | The hidden cost — every yes to something is a quieter no to something else |
| D3 | **Does this make my portfolio more resilient or just more complicated?** | Overextension disguised as safety — pieces that all drain the same bottleneck |

### Excellence (standard, 8.0)

| # | Question | What it catches |
|---|----------|----------------|
| E1 | **Am I pursuing excellence here because I respect the work, or because I'm afraid of what it means about me if I don't?** | The shame engine — dread of mediocrity masquerading as love of craft |
| E2 | **Is "good enough" genuinely not good enough here, or am I the one who can't tolerate it?** | Over-investment — pouring A-game into things that don't need it |
| E3 | **Would I still do it this thoroughly if no one would ever see the result?** | Performance excellence vs. intrinsic excellence — craft for craft's sake vs. craft for the wound |

**Usage rule:** You don't need all 9 every time. Run the **one question from each value that feels most uncomfortable to answer honestly.** Discomfort is signal.

## External Decision Analysis (Questions 2.1–2.6)

When the decision involves other people and stakeholders:

1. **My role** — What is my actual role in making this decision?
2. **Problem map** — What are the problems: root causes vs. symptoms and their relationship?
3. **Stakeholders** — Who are the stakeholders? What is each one's role (minor, major)?
4. **Their stakes** — What are their motivations and fears? What do they expect in best and worst case?
5. **My stakes** — What are my motivations and fears? What do I expect in best and worst case?
6. **Optimization** — The optimal point for win-win and win-lose (I win, they lose)?

---

## Internal Decision Questions

When the decision is about life direction, identity, or "who do I want to be":

1. **What am I afraid of?** — Name it specifically.
2. **What does my body say?** — Chest, shoulders, breathing. Expansion or contraction?
3. **Which option would I choose if nobody was watching?** — Remove the audience entirely.

### PHASED DECISION GATES

> The decision is not binary "freeze or don't." It decomposes into 4 phases, each with its own cost, reversibility, and decision criteria. **The commitment escalates — the decision to proceed at each gate is independent.**

#### Phase 1: Assessment — Turn the lights on (~5% of total cost)

**What:** AFC ultrasound (Day 2-4 of cycle) first → AMH blood test only if AFC is inconclusive → fertility specialist consultation
**Cost:** Lightest — ultrasound + blood draw (if needed) + consultation fee
**Reversibility:** Fully two-way. You learn your numbers and walk away. No physical consequence.
**Why this is the real first decision:** Right now all "knowns" in the GATE are unvalidated. Phase 1 converts population statistics into personal data.

**Doctor's recommended evaluation order (SIH consultation, March 2026):**
1. **AFC first** — transvaginal ultrasound shows the full picture: uterine structure, ovarian characteristics, any pathologies, plus egg count and morphology (size distribution) at that moment
2. **AMH only if needed** — if AFC doesn't give the doctor a clear read on quantity and quality (ratio of mature-sized to immature eggs), then add AMH blood test to measure hormone levels
3. **What standard tests do NOT evaluate:** genetic pathologies the eggs carry; exact hormone levels within individual eggs affecting conception success

**What results mean for me:** Results tell egg quantity (count) and quality (proportion of appropriately-sized eggs vs. immature ones) relative to my biological age bracket. Key insight: biological age may differ from chronological age — a 33-year-old's eggs could reflect the quantity/quality profile of a 23-year-old or a 38-year-old on population health statistics.

**Decision criteria — what the numbers mean for me:**

| AMH result | AFC result | What it signals | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| **≥ 2.0 ng/mL** | **≥ 10 follicles** | Normal/healthy reserve for age 33 | Proceed to Phase 2 evaluation on my timeline — no urgency beyond normal age-related planning. I have breathing room to complete the Compass Check before committing. |
| **1.2 – 2.0 ng/mL** | **5–9 follicles** | Borderline reserve — still workable but declining faster than median | Phase 2 becomes time-sensitive. Evaluate within 3 months. The gap between "now" and "later" matters more for me than for the median 33-year-old. |
| **< 1.2 ng/mL** | **< 5 follicles** | Warning zone — reserve is significantly diminished for age | Urgent specialist deep-dive. Phase 2 decision accelerates. At this level, traditional stimulation may underperform — ask doctor about egg pooling across multiple mini-cycles. |
| **< 0.7 ng/mL** | **< 3 follicles** | Severely diminished reserve | The option-preservation question changes shape entirely. Standard egg freezing may not yield enough viable eggs. Discuss mini-IVF, egg pooling, or whether the cost-benefit still holds. This is where "NO, redirect the investment" becomes a rational answer, not an emotional one. |

**DROP POINT:** After receiving results, I can stop here. The only cost is the consultation. This is the cheapest exit.

**⟹ Phase 1 result (March 2026, SIH):** AFC = 12-15 follicles → **normal/healthy reserve for age 33.** Lands in the top row of the decision table. No AMH blood test needed per doctor's assessment — AFC was conclusive. Proceed to Phase 2 evaluation on my timeline with no urgency beyond normal age-related planning.

#### Phase 2: Retrieval + First Year Storage (~80% of total cost)

**What:** Ovarian stimulation (daily injections for 8 consecutive days, monitoring every 4-5 days) → trigger shot → egg retrieval (10-20 min under light anesthesia — a light 10-min sleep, not deep anesthesia, no significant neurological impact) → vitrification (flash-freezing to -196°C, 90% survival rate per 2024 data, contractually committed) → first year of cryo-storage
**Cost:** 50-60M VND total (from Phase 1 evaluation through thawing). Heaviest single payment — financially and physically.
**Reversibility:** Partially two-way. Money and physical discomfort are sunk. But the eggs just sit in storage — no life commitment made. I can stop paying storage anytime.

**Doctor-confirmed details (SIH, March 2026):**
- **Target harvest:** 12-15 eggs per controlled retrieval
- **Stimulation side effects:** Mainly a concern for patients with pre-existing PCOS (polycystic ovary syndrome) — causes abnormally high egg count → abdominal heaviness, bloating, pain. **I do not have this condition.**
- **Usage at IVF:** 1-2 eggs thawed per attempt (not more, to avoid multiple pregnancies). Unused embryos can be re-frozen.
- **Contract terms:** Best-effort commitment to preserve eggs. No compensation clause if eggs don't survive — only exception is force majeure (war, natural disaster).

**Decision criteria to enter Phase 2:**

| Criterion | Threshold | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Phase 1 numbers support viable yield | AMH ≥ 1.2 AND AFC ≥ 5 | Below this, standard stimulation may not retrieve enough eggs. Discuss pooling strategy with doctor if borderline. |
| Expected egg yield ≥ 10 mature eggs in 1-2 cycles | Doctor's estimate based on my AMH + AFC | At 33, ~10-14 eggs gives ~75% chance of 1 live birth. If my body can produce this in 1-2 cycles, the cost-benefit holds. If it would take 3+ cycles, recalculate. |
| Compass Check completed (at minimum Quick Compass) | S, D, E — no sovereignty violation found | The medical data says "it makes sense." But if the Compass catches that YES is driven by mẹ's expectations, fear of regret, or societal pressure — pause, even if the numbers are good. |
